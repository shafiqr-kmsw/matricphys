Resonant Frequencies in Physics

Short Notes on Stretched Strings, Open Tubes, and Closed Tubes

1. Stretched String Resonance

Wave Velocity in Strings

The velocity of a wave traveling along a stretched string depends on the tension and mass properties of the string.

Wave Velocity Formula:
v = √(T/μ)

Where:
v = wave velocity (m/s)
T = tension in string (N)
μ = linear mass density (kg/m)
Key Point: Linear mass density μ = m/L, where m is the total mass and L is the length of the string.

Resonant Frequencies for Fixed-End Strings

For a string fixed at both ends (like a guitar string), standing wave patterns form with nodes at both ends.

For a string fixed at both ends, standing wave patterns form with nodes at both ends. The fundamental frequency corresponds to one half-wavelength fitting in the string length.

Resonant Frequency Formula:
fn = (n/2L) × √(T/μ)

Where:
n = harmonic number (1, 2, 3, ...)
L = length of string (m)
f1 = fundamental frequency

Example Problem:

A guitar string of length 0.65 m has a mass of 2.0 g and is under 80 N tension. Find the fundamental frequency.

Solution:

μ = 0.002 kg / 0.65 m = 3.08 × 10⁻³ kg/m

v = √(80 / 3.08 × 10⁻³) = 161 m/s

f₁ = v/(2L) = 161/(2 × 0.65) = 124 Hz

2. Open Tube Resonance

Characteristics

An open tube (open at both ends) has antinodes at both ends and supports all harmonics.

An open tube supports standing waves with antinodes at both open ends. All harmonic frequencies are present.

Open Tube Resonant Frequencies:
fn = (n × v)/(2L)

Where:
n = 1, 2, 3, 4, ... (all harmonics present)
v = speed of sound (≈ 343 m/s at 20°C)
L = length of tube
Important: Open tubes support all harmonics. The wavelength of the nth harmonic is λn = 2L/n.

3. Closed Tube Resonance

Characteristics

A closed tube (closed at one end, open at the other) has a node at the closed end and an antinode at the open end. Only odd harmonics are present.

A closed tube has a node at the closed end and an antinode at the open end. Only odd harmonics (1st, 3rd, 5th, etc.) are supported.

Closed Tube Resonant Frequencies:
fn = ((2n-1) × v)/(4L)

Where:
n = 1, 2, 3, ... (gives 1st, 3rd, 5th harmonics)
v = speed of sound
L = length of tube
Key Difference: Closed tubes only produce odd harmonics (1st, 3rd, 5th, ...), while open tubes produce all harmonics.

4. Comparison Summary

System Boundary Conditions Harmonics Present Fundamental Frequency
Fixed String Nodes at both ends All (1, 2, 3, ...) f₁ = v/(2L)
Open Tube Antinodes at both ends All (1, 2, 3, ...) f₁ = v/(2L)
Closed Tube Node at closed end, antinode at open end Odd only (1, 3, 5, ...) f₁ = v/(4L)

6. Practice Problems

Problem 1: String Resonance

A violin string has length 0.33 m, mass 0.5 g, and tension 60 N. Calculate:

a) The wave speed

b) The fundamental frequency

c) The frequency of the 4th harmonic

Solution:

a) μ = 0.0005 kg / 0.33 m = 1.515 × 10⁻³ kg/m

v = √(60 / 1.515 × 10⁻³) = √39,604 = 199 m/s

b) f₁ = v/(2L) = 199/(2 × 0.33) = 199/0.66 = 302 Hz

c) f₄ = 4f₁ = 4 × 302 = 1208 Hz

Problem 2: Open vs Closed Tubes

Compare the fundamental frequencies of:

  • An open tube of length 1.0 m
  • A closed tube of length 1.0 m

Solution:

Open tube: f₁ = 343/(2 × 1.0) = 171.5 Hz

Closed tube: f₁ = 343/(4 × 1.0) = 85.75 Hz

The closed tube has half the fundamental frequency of the open tube of the same length.

Problem 3: Harmonic Analysis

A closed organ pipe produces a fundamental frequency of 220 Hz. What are the frequencies of the next three harmonics?

Solution:

For closed tubes, only odd harmonics exist:

1st harmonic (fundamental): f₁ = 220 Hz

3rd harmonic: f₃ = 3 × 220 = 660 Hz

5th harmonic: f₅ = 5 × 220 = 1100 Hz

7th harmonic: f₇ = 7 × 220 = 1540 Hz

Problem 4: Finding Tube Length

What length of closed tube is needed to produce a fundamental frequency of 440 Hz (A4 note) at 20°C?

Solution:

Using f₁ = v/(4L) and rearranging for L:

L = v/(4f₁) = 343/(4 × 440) = 0.195 m = 19.5 cm

Problem 5: String Tension

A guitar string of length 0.64 m and linear mass density 3.2 × 10⁻³ kg/m needs to produce a fundamental frequency of 330 Hz. What tension is required?

Solution:

From f₁ = (1/2L)√(T/μ), we can solve for T:

T = (2Lf₁)² × μ

T = (2 × 0.64 × 330)² × 3.2 × 10⁻³

T = (422.4)² × 3.2 × 10⁻³ = 178,421 × 3.2 × 10⁻³ = 571 N

Problem 6: Open Tube Harmonics

An open flute has a fundamental frequency of 262 Hz (middle C). Calculate:

a) The length of the flute

b) The frequencies of the 2nd and 3rd harmonics

Solution:

a) L = v/(2f₁) = 343/(2 × 262) = 0.654 m = 65.4 cm

b) 2nd harmonic: f₂ = 2 × 262 = 524 Hz

3rd harmonic: f₃ = 3 × 262 = 786 Hz

7. Harmonics vs Overtones

It's important to understand the distinction between harmonics and overtones, as these terms are often confused.

Harmonics: All frequencies that are integer multiples of the fundamental frequency, including the fundamental itself.
Overtones: All frequencies above the fundamental frequency. The first overtone is the second harmonic, the second overtone is the third harmonic, etc.
Frequency Harmonic Number Overtone Number Example (f₁ = 100 Hz)
f₁ 1st Harmonic (Fundamental) Not an overtone 100 Hz
2f₁ 2nd Harmonic 1st Overtone 200 Hz
3f₁ 3rd Harmonic 2nd Overtone 300 Hz
4f₁ 4th Harmonic 3rd Overtone 400 Hz

Example: Closed Tube Harmonics vs Overtones

A closed tube with fundamental frequency 220 Hz:

Harmonics present: 1st, 3rd, 5th, 7th... (220, 660, 1100, 1540 Hz)

Overtones:

  • 1st overtone = 3rd harmonic = 660 Hz
  • 2nd overtone = 5th harmonic = 1100 Hz
  • 3rd overtone = 7th harmonic = 1540 Hz

Note: The 2nd, 4th, 6th harmonics don't exist in closed tubes, so the overtones skip these frequencies.

8. Key Concepts to Remember

  • Wave Speed: Depends on medium properties (tension/density for strings, temperature for air)
  • Standing Waves: Result from interference between incident and reflected waves
  • Nodes: Points of zero amplitude (destructive interference)
  • Antinodes: Points of maximum amplitude (constructive interference)
  • Harmonics: Integer multiples of the fundamental frequency
  • End Effects: Boundary conditions determine which harmonics are allowed
Exam Tip: Always identify the boundary conditions first, then determine which harmonics are present, and finally apply the appropriate frequency formula.
